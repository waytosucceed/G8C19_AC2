Today’s key takeaways


The new ideas that we learnt from the reading and discussion of ‘The Cop and the Anthem” are as follows:

  1. Irony : Irony typically refers to a discrepancy between appearance and reality. Irony in literature is the purposeful disconnect between the subject matter and the language employed. When a character's point of view diverges from the narrator's or reader's, irony ensues.
    ‘Soapy’s spirit was proud’ The given sentence is ironic.
    Soapy was living on the streets, but he felt that living as a vagrant would damage his pride, so he decided to stay in the prison reserved for criminals.

  2. Deus ex machina : The anthem acts as deus ex machina which induced the epiphany in Soapy and caused his self-introspection. ‘deus ex machina’ is a latin phrase which refers to ‘godly machinery’ or ‘godly intervention’ that helps to resolve a problem or entangle a plot. The anthem is associated with the church which means it is associated with God. If the sweet music of the familiar anthem had not reached Soapy’s ears and reminded him of the days when he knew the anthem so well, Soapy would not have been moved by it to an extent such that he experienced a change in him. As a result he intricately and deeply introspects himself.

  3. Situational Irony : Situational irony is when the outcome is the opposite or completely different from what was expected.Situational irony contradicts reasonable expectations and logical cause-and-effect linkages. A situation like this appears "unfair".
    Situational irony is characterised by this feeling of being "unfair" or "unfortunate." People start to wonder if the universe makes sense because they are unable to explain the unfairness.The ending of the story is indeed ironic for Soapy as well as the readers. Soapy had been dreaming of prison life for the winter and the entire day he was literally imposing himself on cops to get arrested.
    However, when he was returning to Madison square with a frustrated mind, the anthem which is a godly intervention induces him to change himself completely to an extent that he was now at peace with himself with a rewired mind and better hopes for the future. Blackwell’s Island is no more his dream now. It is O. Henry’s adept use of situational irony that fulfilled Soapy’s dream of his immediate past right in front of the church.
